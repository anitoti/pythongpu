\begin{frame}{The Puzzle: Stability and Nimbleness at the Same Time}
  \GeoPanels
  \vspace{0.3cm}
  Your brain holds a thought steadily --- and drops it the instant something
  relevant happens. Those two demands fight each other.
  \vspace{0.35cm}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.47\textwidth}
      \begin{block}{Too stable}
        A state you cannot leave without a large push. Robust, but rigid ---
        you would not notice the fire alarm.
      \end{block}
    \column{0.47\textwidth}
      \begin{block}{Too unstable}
        A state that noise destroys on its own. Responsive, but you could not
        hold a thought for a second.
      \end{block}
  \end{columns}
  \vspace{0.35cm}
  The brain does both, in milliseconds, with \textbf{no central controller}
  deciding when to switch. Whatever resolves this tension has to be built into
  the \emph{geometry} of the system itself.
  \vfill
  {\footnotesize\color{Rust}\textit{``Small changes in current state caused by environmental
  stimuli would be enough to switch between distinct stable brain states.''}
  \hfill --- Bollt, Fish, et al.}
\end{frame}
\note{Don't rush this. The tension is the hook: stability and sensitivity are opposites, and
the brain refuses to choose. No homunculus is flipping the switch. So the resolution must be
structural. ~1 min.}

\begin{frame}{The Proposed Answer, in One Picture}
  \GeoPanels
  \vspace{0.2cm}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.52\textwidth}
      \vspace{0.2cm}
      Picture a marble on a landscape of valleys. Each \textbf{valley} is a brain
      state; where the marble rolls depends on where you drop it.
      \medskip

      If the \textbf{ridgelines} are smooth, you need a big shove to change valleys.
      \medskip

      But if the ridgelines are infinitely crinkly --- \textbf{fractal} --- then
      almost wherever you stand, an edge is a hair's breadth away. A whisper of a
      stimulus moves you.
      \medskip

      {\color{Rust}\textbf{Crinkliness is nimbleness.}} That is the hypothesis
      this project exists to test.
    \column{0.44\textwidth}
      \centering
      \fig{basin_cartoon.png}{Smooth boundary (left): hard to switch.
      Fractal boundary (right): always poised at an edge.}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}
\note{This is the one mental image the audience must leave with. Say it slowly; draw the
ridgeline in the air with your hand. Everything technical after this is just: how do you
measure crinkliness, and how do you know you measured it and not an illusion. ~1 min.}

\begin{frame}{Why It Matters Beyond Neuroscience}
  \GeoPanels
  \vspace{0.25cm}
  The same geometry governs any system of coupled oscillators that has to be
  both reliable and switchable:
  \vspace{0.3cm}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.31\textwidth}
      \begin{block}{Brain dynamics}
        Rapid task switching; and pathological \emph{failures} to switch ---
        seizures are a network locking into one state.
      \end{block}
    \column{0.31\textwidth}
      \begin{block}{Power grids}
        Generators must stay phase-locked. Which disturbances tip the grid out of
        sync is exactly a basin-boundary question.
      \end{block}
    \column{0.31\textwidth}
      \begin{block}{Predictability itself}
        When boundaries are fractal, more precise measurement buys you almost
        \emph{no} extra predictive power. That is a hard limit, not an engineering
        problem.
      \end{block}
  \end{columns}
  \vspace{0.35cm}
  \centering
  The mathematics does not know it is in a skull. If fractal basins are generic in
  coupled networks, ``nimbleness'' is a property of \textbf{wiring}, not of neurons.
\end{frame}
\note{Answers "why should I care" for the non-neuro people. The predictability point is the
deep one: fractal basins mean a fundamental obstruction, not a measurement problem. ~1 min.}

\begin{frame}{Vocabulary: Attractor, Basin, Basin Map}
  \GeoPanels
  \vspace{0.2cm}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.55\textwidth}
      \begin{block}{Attractor {\normalfont(the valley)}}
        A compact set $A$, invariant under the flow ($\varphi_t(A)=A$), with an
        open neighbourhood $U$ such that every trajectory starting in $U$
        converges to $A$; and \emph{minimal} --- no proper piece of it is itself
        attracting.
      \end{block}
      \begin{block}{Basin {\normalfont(where you must drop the marble)}}
        \[ \mathcal{B}(A)=\{\,\mathbf{x}_0 : \omega(\mathbf{x}_0)\subseteq A\,\} \]
        The set of initial conditions whose long-run limit is $A$.
      \end{block}
    \column{0.42\textwidth}
      \begin{block}{Basin map {\normalfont(the picture)}}
        Sample a grid of initial conditions, colour each by \emph{which} attractor
        it reaches. Real basins appear as \textbf{connected regions}. The
        boundaries between colours are the object we measure.
      \end{block}
      \begin{block}{\alert{The fact that matters most}}
        Fix the parameters and the system \emph{has} some definite number of
        attractors. It is a property of the equations --- not a setting we choose.
        \textbf{We are estimating an integer that is already out there.}
      \end{block}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}
\note{Give the real definition, then immediately translate: attractor = valley, basin = which
starting points roll there, basin map = the colored picture. The last box is the seed of the
entire second half — the count is a FACT, not a knob. ~1.5 min.}

\begin{frame}{Vocabulary: Fractal Boundary --- and an Ambiguity}
  \GeoPanels
  \vspace{0.2cm}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.52\textwidth}
      \textbf{Box-counting dimension.} Cover the boundary with boxes of side
      $\varepsilon$; let $N(\varepsilon)$ be the number of boxes it touches. If
      $N(\varepsilon)\sim\varepsilon^{-D}$, then
      \[ D_0=\lim_{\varepsilon\to0}\frac{\log N(\varepsilon)}{\log(1/\varepsilon)} \]
      \smallskip
      Smooth curve $\Rightarrow D=1$. Area-filling $\Rightarrow D=2$.
      A fractal boundary lives strictly between.
      \smallskip
      In practice: measure $N$ at a ladder of $\varepsilon$, and read $D$ off the
      slope of $\log N$ vs $\log(1/\varepsilon)$.
    \column{0.44\textwidth}
      \begin{block}{\alert{Remember this for slide 11}}
        $D\to2$ has \textbf{two} explanations:
        \smallskip
        \begin{enumerate}
          \item a genuinely, intricately interwoven boundary --- the exciting one;
          \item \textbf{a random labelling}, where every pixel borders another
                colour, so the ``boundary'' fills the plane.
        \end{enumerate}
        \smallskip
        Both give $D\approx2$ with a beautiful straight-line fit.
        \textbf{$D$ alone cannot tell them apart.}
      \end{block}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}
\note{Plant the landmine explicitly and tell them you're planting it: "hold onto this, it
detonates in slide 11." The failure mode and the triumph look identical in D. ~1.5 min.}

% ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════ 7
\begin{frame}{The Theorems We Lean On}
  \GeoPanels
  \vspace{0.2cm}
  \begin{block}{Uncertainty exponent \hfill {\normalfont Grebogi, McDonald, Ott \& Yorke (1983)}}
    Perturb a random initial condition by $\varepsilon$ and ask how often the final
    state changes. Let $f(\varepsilon)$ be that fraction of ``$\varepsilon$-uncertain''
    points. Then $f(\varepsilon)\sim\varepsilon^{\alpha}$, and the boundary dimension
    obeys
    \[ \boxed{\;D = d - \alpha\;}\qquad (d=2\ \text{for our slice}) \]
    \footnotesize
    Interpretation: improving your precision by $10\times$ reduces your chance of
    being wrong by only $10^{\alpha}$. As $\alpha\to0$ the boundary becomes
    space-filling and extra precision buys nothing --- \emph{final-state sensitivity}.
  \end{block}
  \vspace{0.1cm}
  \begin{block}{Basin entropy \hfill {\normalfont Daza, Wagemakers, Georgeot, Gu\'ery-Odelin \& Sanju\'an (2016)}}
    Cover the map with $\varepsilon$-boxes. In box $i$, with basin frequencies
    $p_{i,j}$, the Gibbs entropy is $S_i=-\sum_j p_{i,j}\ln p_{i,j}$. Average over
    \emph{boundary} boxes to get $S_{bb}$. Then
    \[ \boxed{\;S_{bb} > \ln 2 \;\Longrightarrow\; \text{the boundary is fractal}\;} \]
    \footnotesize
    A \emph{sufficient condition} --- a theorem, not a slope fit. This is where the
    project should go next.
  \end{block}
\end{frame}
\note{Two results that do real work. GMOY gives us D = d - alpha, so alpha near 0 means
D near 2 means predictability collapses. Daza gives a *sufficient condition* for fractality
that doesn't depend on fitting a line. Say out loud: a theorem beats a slope fit. ~1.5 min.}

\begin{frame}{The System I Study}
  \GeoPanels
  \vspace{0.2cm}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.56\textwidth}
      Each of $N=83$ brain regions is a chaotic \textbf{Lorenz} oscillator,
      \[
        \dot X=\sigma(Y-X),\quad \dot Y=X(\rho-Z)-Y,\quad \dot Z=XY-\beta Z
      \]
      with the classic chaotic parameters $\sigma=10,\ \rho=28,\ \beta=\tfrac83$.
      \medskip

      The regions are wired by a real human \textbf{DTI} scan. From its adjacency
      matrix $A$ we build the graph Laplacian $L=\mathrm{diag}(A\mathbf{1})-A$
      and couple through it:
      \[
        \boxed{\;\dot{\mathbf{x}}_i=f(\mathbf{x}_i)\;-\;K\sum_{j=1}^{N}L_{ij}\,H\mathbf{x}_j\;}
      \]
      \footnotesize
      $H=e_1e_1^{\!\top}$ couples the $x$-components only; $K\ge0$ is the coupling
      strength --- \textbf{the one knob I turn}.
    \column{0.40\textwidth}
      \begin{block}{The numbers}
        \footnotesize
        \begin{tabular}{ll}
          \toprule
          Regions $N$ & 83 \\
          Edges & 850 \\
          State space & $83\times3=249$ dims \\
          Coupling $K$ & swept, $0 \to 0.65$ \\
          \bottomrule
        \end{tabular}
      \end{block}
      \begin{block}{Why Laplacian coupling}
        \footnotesize
        $L\mathbf{1}=\mathbf{0}$, so the fully synchronised state is \emph{always}
        a solution, at every $K$. The question is whether it is the \emph{only}
        one --- i.e.\ whether other attractors coexist.
      \end{block}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}
\note{The Laplacian point is worth 15 seconds: row sums are zero, so synchrony is always an
exact solution. The interesting physics is whether anything ELSE survives alongside it.
That framing makes the eventual "we only find one attractor" result meaningful rather than
disappointing. ~1.5 min.}

\begin{frame}{The Fingerprint --- and the Step That Cannot Say ``No''}
  \GeoPanels
  \vspace{0.2cm}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.54\textwidth}
      \textbf{The problem.} Two initial conditions on the \emph{same} chaotic
      attractor produce completely different trajectories. So I cannot label a run
      by comparing time series --- I need something \textbf{constant on an attractor}.
      \medskip

      \textbf{The fix (VPS).} For every pair of nodes $(i,j)$, take two
      \emph{time-averaged} quantities: a phase-coherence measure and an amplitude
      relation. Stack all $\binom{83}{2}=3403$ pairs:
      \[ \mathbf{e}\in\mathbb{R}^{6806}\ \text{per initial condition.} \]
      Time averages converge on an attractor $\Rightarrow$ same attractor, same
      fingerprint. \checkmark
    \column{0.42\textwidth}
      \begin{block}{\alert{Then: $k$-means}}
        Cluster the fingerprints; each cluster becomes one colour on the map.
        \medskip
        $k$-means \textbf{always returns exactly $k$ groups}. Hand it structureless
        data and it does not object --- it hands back $k$ confident, meaningless
        basins.
        \medskip
        \textbf{An estimator that cannot fail cannot be evidence.}
      \end{block}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}
\note{Two beats. VPS is legitimately clever and legitimately correct — time averages are
attractor-invariant. The clustering step is where the trouble is, and it's not a coding bug,
it's a conceptual one. ~1.5 min.}

\begin{frame}{Hypothesis}
  \GeoPanels
  \vspace{0.35cm}
  \begin{block}{H}
    \centering\normalsize
    \textbf{$D_f\approx1.9$ is not measuring a fractal boundary. It is what
    box-counting returns when a clustering is \emph{imposed} on features that do
    not separate.}
  \end{block}
  \vspace{0.25cm}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.46\textwidth}
      \begin{block}{The mechanism}
        \footnotesize
        If the labels are pixel-level noise, then \emph{every} pixel borders
        another colour. The boundary is space-filling, so
        \[ D_f\to 2,\qquad \alpha = 2-D_f \to 0. \]
        We measured $D_f\approx1.96,\ \alpha\approx0.04$. That is not evidence
        \emph{for} an interwoven boundary --- it is \textbf{equally} the signature
        of noise.
      \end{block}
    \column{0.50\textwidth}
      \begin{block}{Four predictions, if H is true}
        \footnotesize
        \begin{description}\setlength\itemsep{2pt}
          \item[P1] the basin-count selector is fitting a knee that does not exist
          \item[P2] the VPS features contain no separable structure
          \item[P3] a method \emph{allowed} to answer ``one attractor'' will answer one
          \item[P4] an \textbf{independent} descriptor will agree
        \end{description}
      \end{block}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}
\note{This slide is why this is science and not just debugging. A falsifiable claim, four
predictions that could each have come out the other way. Slow down here. ~1.5 min.}

% ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════ 13
\begin{frame}{P1 --- The Elbow Fits a Corner That Isn't There}
  \GeoPanels
  \vspace{0.2cm}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.54\textwidth}
      \textbf{How ``how many basins?'' was being answered.} Cluster at each $k$;
      let $W(k)$ be the within-cluster sum of squares. $W$ always decreases, so you
      cannot minimise it --- instead find the ``corner'' of diminishing returns
      (Kneedle: the point farthest from the chord joining the endpoints).
      \medskip

      \textbf{Why it fails.} A corner exists only if the data \emph{has}
      well-separated groups. On a near-degenerate cloud, $W(k)$ decays smoothly
      with no corner --- but Kneedle \alert{never abstains}. Given any convex
      decreasing curve it returns a point in the middle of the search range.
      \medskip

      {\color{Rust}\textbf{So ``6--8 basins'' was a fact about the range $[2,15]$,
      not about the brain.}}
    \column{0.42\textwidth}
      \begin{block}{The conceptual error}
        The number of attractors is fixed by the vector field. It changes only at
        bifurcations. It is not ours to choose.
        \medskip
        $k$ is an \textbf{estimator} of that integer. Sweeping $k$ is honest
        sensitivity analysis. Letting a broken heuristic pick $k$ and then
        reporting the resulting $D_f$ as physics is \textbf{measuring an artifact}.
      \end{block}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}
\note{"The elbow always answers, even when the honest answer is 'there's nothing here.'"
Tie back to slide 5: the count is a fact about the equations. ~1.5 min.}

% ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════ 14
\begin{frame}{P2 --- Building a Test That \emph{Can} Say ``Nothing Here''}
  \GeoPanels
  \vspace{0.15cm}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.53\textwidth}
      \textbf{The statistic.} Let $s^\star$ be the best clustering quality
      (silhouette) achievable on the real features, and compare it to the same
      quantity on a \textbf{structureless reference} $Z$ drawn from a single
      Gaussian matched to the data's own mean and covariance:
      \[ \Delta \;=\; s^\star_{\text{real}} \;-\; Q_{95}\big(s^\star_{Z}\big) \]
      Declare structure only if $\Delta\ge$ margin. The reference is
      \textbf{unimodal by construction} but has the real elliptical spread --- so
      genuine basins must beat ``one blob,'' not a strawman.
      \medskip

      Only \textbf{balanced} partitions count: a split isolating five outlier
      points is not a basin.
    \column{0.44\textwidth}
      \begin{block}{Validated where I know the answer}
        \centering\footnotesize
        \begin{tabular}{lcc}
          \toprule
          synthetic input & $\Delta$ & verdict \\
          \midrule
          3 real clusters & $+0.38$ & $k=3$ \checkmark \\
          one blob & $-0.01$ & none \checkmark \\
          blob $+$ outliers & $-0.26$ & none \checkmark \\
          heavy-tailed blob & $-0.00$ & none \checkmark \\
          \bottomrule
        \end{tabular}
        \par\smallskip
        {\footnotesize On one-blob data the null reproduces the data's own score
        ($\Delta\approx0$); real structure clears it by $+0.38$.}
      \end{block}
      \begin{block}{\alert{Three of my own bugs}}
        \footnotesize
        My first version was \emph{wrong}: its null scored $0.94$ where it should
        have scored $\approx0$, and it declared ``structure'' on margins of
        $0.018$. I only caught it by testing on data with known answers.
      \end{block}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}
\note{Own the bugs out loud — it's the most credible thing in the talk. Outliers dominated;
the reference had the wrong shape; and the threshold was smaller than the statistic's own
run-to-run scatter. Lesson: build the ruler, and check the ruler, before trusting the
measurement. ~2 min.}

\begin{frame}{P3 \& P4 --- Two Independent Counts}
  \GeoPanels
  \vspace{0.15cm}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.49\textwidth}
      \begin{block}{P3 --- let the count emerge}
        \footnotesize
        Density-based clustering (DBSCAN): groups are dense regions separated by
        sparse ones. It is outlier-robust and \textbf{allowed to answer one}.
        Run on the saved features of all 20 couplings:
        \smallskip
        \centering\textbf{one attractor at every $K$}\\
        {\scriptsize stable in 18/20; the $K=0$ uncoupled control correctly returns 1}
      \end{block}
      \begin{block}{P4 --- throw away VPS entirely}
        \footnotesize
        Re-integrated 7 couplings and counted from \textbf{final-state statistics}
        instead: no VPS, no $k$-means, no silhouette. Shares \emph{no machinery}
        with everything above.
      \end{block}
    \column{0.47\textwidth}
      \begin{block}{\alert{P4 did not confirm P3}}
        \centering\footnotesize
        \begin{tabular}{lcc}
          \toprule
          $K$ & count & count as radius grows \\
          \midrule
          0.00 & 1 & 2 $\to$ 1 $\to$ 1 \\
          0.10 & 1 & 38 $\to$ 1 $\to$ 1 \\
          0.20 & 39 & 47 $\to$ 39 $\to$ 2 \\
          0.45 & 6 & 64 $\to$ 6 $\to$ 2 \\
          0.65 & 11 & 45 $\to$ 11 $\to$ 5 \\
          \bottomrule
        \end{tabular}
        \par\smallskip
        {\footnotesize \textbf{Not one stable count.} Doubling the threshold moves
        the answer from 64 to 2. Well-separated attractors would hold a
        \textbf{plateau} --- real density gaps don't care about your threshold.
        There is none. This is a \textbf{continuum}, so I report \emph{none} of
        these numbers.}
      \end{block}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}
\note{Be scrupulous here: P4 DISAGREES with P3 on the numbers. What they agree on is that
there's nothing robust to find. Don't oversell. The plateau argument is the honest reading,
and it's why I refuse to put "39 attractors" on a slide. ~2 min.}

\begin{frame}{The Result}
  \GeoPanels
  \vspace{0.25cm}
  \begin{block}{What I can defend}
    \centering
    \textbf{In these 2-D slices of the DTI-coupled Lorenz network, for
    $K\in[0,0.65]$, the data do not support multiple coexisting basins. The
    reported $D_f\approx1.9$ is the signature of box-counting a \emph{partition of
    noise}.}
  \end{block}
  \vspace{0.15cm}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.47\textwidth}
      \begin{block}{Converging evidence}
        \footnotesize
        \begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{1pt}
          \item the basin map has no connected regions at any scale
          \item $D_f$ is flat in $K$ --- no bifurcation signature
          \item $D_f\to2$, $\alpha\to0$: the space-filling fingerprint
          \item emergent counting says \textbf{one}, not 6--8
          \item an independent descriptor finds \textbf{no plateau} at any $K$
          \item the $K=0$ control behaves correctly
        \end{itemize}
      \end{block}
    \column{0.49\textwidth}
      \begin{block}{\alert{What I am not claiming}}
        \footnotesize
        \begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{1pt}
          \item \textbf{Not} that fractal basins don't exist. The Bollt--Fish
                mechanism is untouched by this.
          \item \textbf{Not} a statement about other models, other slices, other
                couplings.
          \item This is \textbf{absence of evidence}: VPS may simply fail to
                \emph{resolve} attractors that are there.
          \item A 2-D slice through a 249-D space may intersect only one basin.
        \end{itemize}
      \end{block}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Why This Generalises}
  \GeoPanels
  \vspace{0.4cm}
  \begin{center}
    Any pipeline shaped like\\[0.3cm]
    {\large\ttfamily cluster $\longrightarrow$ box-count $\longrightarrow$ report $D$}\\[0.3cm]
    can turn pure noise into a confident $D\approx2$.
  \end{center}
  \vspace{0.3cm}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \column{0.47\textwidth}
      \begin{block}{Because}
        the clusterer cannot abstain --- and $D\to2$ \emph{reads} as a triumph
        (``maximally interwoven!'') rather than as a failure.
      \end{block}
    \column{0.47\textwidth}
      \begin{block}{So the gate must come first}
        Structure test $\to$ emergent count $\to$ robustness check. Only then is
        a $D_f$, a basin entropy, or a Wada result worth reporting.
      \end{block}
  \end{columns}
  \vfill
  \centering{\color{Rust}\textbf{The most dangerous bug is the one that makes your
  result look better.}}
\end{frame}
\note{The transferable lesson, and the strongest line in the talk. Land the last sentence and
pause. ~1 min.}
